Gravitational Force

The force due to gravity is given by the same simple equation as in other projectile physics problems: \(F_{G} = mg\). However, in this case, calculating the gravitational acceleration and the mass of the rocket introduces new problems.

As in the air density and drag definitions, you have a few options (in increasing order of difficulty and accuracy):

  1. Assume \(g\) and rocket total mass is constant.
  2. Assume \(g\) is constant but the rocket total mass varying over time.
  3. Allow \(g\) to vary with height and allow the rocket total mass to vary over time.

First, the gravitational acceleration at an altitude \(z\) from the Earth's surface is given by $$ g = \frac{G M_{E}}{\left(z+R_{E}\right)^{2}} $$ where

  • \(G\) is the gravitational constant
  • \(R_{E}\) is the radius of the Earth (6,371,000 m)
  • \(M_{E}\) is the mass of the Earth (\(5.972 \times 10^{24} \mathrm{kg}\)).

The mass of the rocket will always be decreasing as it expels fuel. Continuing to assume constant thrust, and therefore a constant mass flow rate out of the rocket, we can model the mass of the rocket with a linear time dependence:

$$ m = m_0 - \dot{m} \times t $$

where

  • the mass flow rate, \(\dot{m}\), was computed in the thrust force task.

Then the gravitational force is simply

$$ F_{G} = m \times g $$

Because mass is decreasing over time, and gravity is decreasing with altitude, then gravitational force depends on time and altitude.
