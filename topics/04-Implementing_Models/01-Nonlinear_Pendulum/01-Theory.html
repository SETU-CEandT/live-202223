Background Theory

In this page, we define all of our notation and specify the problem under study.

Nonlinear Problem

Our starting point is the second order differential equation

\[ \frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} = -\frac{g}{L} \sin(\theta) \] where dependant variable, \(\theta\) is the angle the pendulum makes with the vertical axis at time, \(t\), and

  • \(g\) is acceleration due to gravity (here assumed to be 9.81\(\text{m}/\text{s}^2\)
  • \(L\) is the length of the rod

and initial conditions of

  • initial position, \(\theta(0)=A\).
  • initial velocity, \(\dot\theta(0)=B\).

Linear Problem

Under the assumption that the angle, \(\theta\), is small we use Taylor's series to get an approximation for \(\sin(\theta)\) as follows:

\[ \sin(\theta) = \theta + \underbrace{-\frac{\theta^2}{2!}+\frac{\theta^4}{4!}+ \cdots}_{\text{small}} \approx \theta \]

The non-linear problem then reduces to the following linear problem

\[ \frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} = -\frac{g}{L} \theta \]

which, on defining \(\omega=\sqrt{\frac{g}{L}} \) we get the more familiar

\[ \frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} + \omega^2 \theta = 0 \]

This has, as you know, analytical solution of

\[ \theta(t) = A \cos(\omega t) + \frac{B}{\omega} \sin(\omega t) \]
